# WiFi Years – Learning Wireless the Hard Way

## Spain, 2007

In 2007 I moved to Spain to live with my mom.

I brought my own HDD with me, but there was no computer in the house.  
So the first thing I asked for was a **laptop and an HDD dock** so I could access my files.

After a few weeks I got bored.

There was only TV in the house.  
No internet.  
Mom didn't see any reason to pay for it.

But when I checked the laptop’s WiFi list I saw **several networks around the building**.

So naturally I started thinking:

*How do I connect to those?*

I asked during a Spanish class I attended once a week if anyone knew how to connect to a neighbour's WiFi. One guy said:

> "I'll bring you a CD next week."

---

## Discovering Wifislax

The next week he brought me a CD with **Wifislax**.

It was a Linux-based OS designed for **wardriving and wireless testing**.

I asked him which tool I should use.

He said:

> "Look for WEP first."

That was the entire explanation.

---

## First Experiments

For the next few days I started exploring everything inside Wifislax.

I didn't really know what each tool did yet, so I just experimented with them one by one.

Soon I noticed several tools specifically focused on **WEP networks**.

Eventually I managed to get into one.

After that I started reading **Wifislax forums**, learning what each tool actually did and how wireless authentication worked.

---

## Backup Networks

The only problem with the first network I used was that the owner **turned it off every weekend**.

So I needed a backup.

Over the next weeks I managed to access several more networks.

Some were **WEP**, some were **WPA**.

At one point I had around **five different networks** I could switch between.

That setup basically gave me internet for **about a year and a half while I lived in Valencia**.

---

## Ukraine – Repair Shop Years

After Spain I moved back to Ukraine. I was around 20.

I tried different jobs:

- database operations at a city newspaper  
- some design work  

A year later I opened a **repair station for phones, tablets, PCs and laptops**.

I worked there self‑employed for about **four years**.

Eventually the owner sold the building where the shop was located.  
I moved to another place but it never worked well, so I decided to close the shop.

By that time I already had a second job doing:

- **video editing**
- **website administration**

for a local business, remotely.

---

## Crimea, 2013

In 2013 some friends suggested going to work in **Crimea** for the season as a waiter or bartender.

I agreed.

We found a short-term apartment and later tried to rent something long-term.

But when we finally found a place we forgot to ask an important question:

**Is there WiFi?**

There wasn't.

The apartment belonged to an elderly woman who didn't use the internet.

So once again, **Wifislax returned to the story**.

This time I remember running tools for **handshake capture and brute force attacks**.

At one point I launched **Reaver**, left the laptop running, and went to sleep.

When I woke up my friends were standing around my laptop smiling while the password was being recovered.

---

## Back to Spain

A few years later, when the war in Donbass started, I moved back to Spain to stay with my mom for a while.

She decided to rent another apartment so my partner and I could live there too.

The problem was that installing internet could take **months**.

So again, I opened Wifislax.

At that point the process already felt very familiar.  
Within minutes I managed to get access to a couple of nearby networks.

---

## Looking Back

Those years were my **unofficial introduction to wireless networks**.

Not through textbooks or courses — but through curiosity, experimentation, and a lot of trial and error.

Wifislax, forums, and real networks around me were basically my first lab environment.

Looking back now, that period probably shaped my interest in **systems, networks, and how security actually breaks in practice**.
